&tex=The Moche people were highly skilled in many different crafts and we have many examples of the objects they created such as <font color="#FFCC66"><u><a href="javascript:openGlos('glossary/c/ceramics.html','glos')">ceramics</a></u></font>, metalwork and wood carvings. One of the areas in which they were accomplished was <font color="#FFCC66"><u><a href="javascript:openGlos('glossary/w/weaving.html','glos')">weaving</a></u></font>. Woven cloth was used for a variety of purposes including everyday clothing and <font color="#FFCC66"><u><a href="javascript:openGlos('glossary/c/ceremonial.html','glos')">ceremonial</a></u></font> outfits. 
Unlike other ancient Peruvian cultures where many <font color="#FFCC66"><u><a href="javascript:openGlos('glossary/t/textiles.html','glos')">textiles</a></u></font> have been <font color="#FFCC66"><u><a href="javascript:openGlos('glossary/p/preserved.html','glos')">preserved</a></u></font> in desert graves, very few examples of actual Moche textiles survive today. Thus it is difficult to know about the colours and patterns they would have used. However, there is some archaeological evidence of how they looked, including examples of clothing and cloth shown on pottery vessels.
